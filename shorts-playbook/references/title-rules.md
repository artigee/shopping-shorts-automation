# Title / Caption Rules

On-screen titles tend to come out *good* for the same reason VO comes out *generic*: titles are constrained, VO isn't. The title form forces compression, rhythm, and attitude. Preserve that constraint.

## Rule 1 — The title carries the claim or spec

The title states what the thing IS or DOES: the number, the feature, the price, the result. Fragment form is good — it forces the squeeze.

- "No ice. No salt. Zero setup."
- "20 min → real ice cream"
- "Single-serve. No machine babysitting."

## Rule 2 — Title ≠ VO (the split test)

The title and the VO for the same beat must never say the same thing. Title = claim. VO = reaction the title can't carry.

**Test:** If the VO line is just the title expanded into a full sentence, the beat is broken. Re-author the VO to react, not restate.

- ❌ Title "20 min ⏱" / VO "Twenty minutes later it's done." (restates)
- ✅ Title "20 min ⏱" / VO "Twenty minutes. I timed it, because of course I did." (reacts)

## Rule 3 — Compression

Titles are squeezed, not written. Cut articles and connectors. Aim for 2–6 words or a tight fragment. Punctuation as rhythm ("Pour. Snap. One button.") is good. An arrow (`→`) compresses a before/after into one line.

## Rule 4 — Derive titles FROM the VO monologue

After the continuous VO is written, derive the titles as compression of the key beats. The VO is the through-line; the titles are the squeeze of it. This keeps them in sync without making either narrate the other.

## Rule 5 — CTA caption is short and not salesy

The final caption states the mechanic plainly. Keep the salesy register out — that lives nowhere in this skill.

- ✅ "Comment NEED IT 👇"
- ❌ "Comment below and I'll drop the link straight to your DMs!!" (banned phrasing — see banlist)
