# Storytelling — impact & ear-catching

The structure decides what happens; THIS file decides whether anyone keeps listening. Goal: every line earns the next second of attention. Edit freely — this is the dial for "more impactful / ear-catching."

## 0. Fit the time — the hard limit
A line must be SPEAKABLE in its scene. Calm pace ≈ 2.5 words/second. So a scene's VO words ≤ duration × 2.5: a 4s scene = ~10 words MAX, a 2s scene = ~5 words. Count the words. A line nobody can finish in time is worse than a short one. The whole monologue must fit the total video length (a 16s video ≈ 40 words TOTAL). Short shorts use FEW words — write tight, then cut.

### The duration rule (total → per-shot → words)

- **Total length** defaults to the **reference reel's measured length** (editable). The sum of the shots' seconds should land close to it.
- **Estimate a WEIGHT per shot, not seconds.** Weight = how much screen time the shot deserves vs the others, judged by ROLE (hook punchy/low; setup/connective low; reveal/turn + payoff high; CTA slightly high), the HOOK shape, the PERSONA's cadence, and the reel's PACING — never an even split. The app converts weights → seconds so they sum to the total (clamped per shot).
- **Words follow the weight's share:** each shot's VO ≤ `(total × weight ÷ Σweights) × 2.5` words. Heavier shots get more words; the hook and connective shots are terse.
- ❌ (4s) "I did not believe a travel cot was going to be the thing I actually use every single trip — we have a graveyard of gear that sounded good at two in the morning." (~32 words — impossible in 4s)
- ✅ (4s) "Swore I'd never use a travel cot. Four trips later." (~10 words)

## 1. Cold open — land the hook in 1.5 seconds
The first VO line is a cold open. No throat-clearing ("So", "Okay so", "I want to talk about", "Let me tell you"). Start mid-action, mid-thought, or mid-confession. Lead with the most surprising, most specific, or most relatable thing — a number, a contradiction, a small shame.
- ❌ "So I found this cool travel cot on Amazon."
- ✅ "I've packed this exact cot four trips in a row now."

## 2. Write for the EAR, not the page
Read every line out loud. If you stumble or run out of breath, cut it. Spoken rhythm beats grammar.
- **Vary length.** Short punch, then a longer line, then a fragment. Sameness is what makes VO sound like a bot.
- At least **two lines per video are short fragments** (2–5 words): "That's the whole trick." / "No notes." / "Still can't believe it."
- A 2-word line hits hardest right after a long one.

## 3. Tension, then release — don't resolve in the same breath
Set up a doubt or a question, hold it for a beat, THEN pay it off. The gap is what holds attention.
- ❌ "It snaps on in one second and works great."
- ✅ "It snaps on — and I kept waiting for that to be the annoying part. It just… wasn't."

## 3b. Withhold the product — the reveal IS the payoff (this is a SHOPPING short)
This is a shopping short, not a story for its own sake: the job is to stop the scroll and earn the **DM/comment**. The product reveal is the **payoff**, never the setup.
- The **hook and the early build sell the PROBLEM / doubt / curiosity — NOT the product.** Do not name, hero, or fully explain the product (or its exact mechanism) in scene 1–2.
- If you hand over the product + how it works in the first few seconds, the open loop closes and there's no reason left to keep watching or to DM. That is the #1 way a shopping short leaks its reason-to-reach-out.
- The product **enters at the turn** as the answer to the loop, lands at the payoff, then the CTA converts.
- ❌ scene 2: "The retinol duo — one damages, one repairs." (reveal + mechanism in 4s → loop closed, scroll gone)
- ✅ build the "why did my skin freak out?" loop first; reveal the fix / product only as the turn.

## 4. One clean TURN
Every good short has one line where it flips: doubt → belief, problem → fix, "that's it?" → payoff. Give that line its own beat. Don't bury it mid-sentence.

## 5. Specific obliterates generic
One concrete detail outperforms any adjective. Numbers, named choices, tiny confessions.
- ❌ "It's really convenient and works so well."
- ✅ "Set it up next to the bed in about forty seconds. I timed it."

## 5b. No ambiguous shorthand on screen
A title/line must read clean in ONE second. Avoid shorthand that can be misread out of context. Prefer the concrete, unambiguous word.
- ❌ "Four states. One bag." ("states" can read as states-of-matter / conditions for a beat)
- ✅ "Four trips. One bag." / "Four cities. One bag."
If a compressed word has a second meaning a viewer could trip on, swap it for the plain one.

## 6. Cut every word that isn't pulling weight
Delete "basically", "literally", "honestly", "just", "really", "very" unless the persona's voice truly needs it. Filler is the enemy of impact. If a line still lands with a word removed, remove it.

## 7. The closing VO IS a casual CTA — never drop the ask
The last spoken line must still ASK: comment the keyword to get the link. Keep it low-key and confident, not a hard sell — but the ask has to be there (the creator says it out loud in the real reels).
- ✅ "Say the word and it's in your DMs." / "Comment 'want it,' I'll send it your way."
- ❌ a verdict with no ask: "Bare minimum. It delivered. No notes." (that's a sign-off, not a CTA — the viewer isn't told to do anything)
A standalone verdict/sign-off can live in the SECOND-to-last beat; the final beat's VO is the casual spoken CTA.

## 8. Plant an open loop in line 1
Open a question the viewer needs answered, and DON'T answer it until the payoff. The unresolved gap is the single biggest retention lever — the brain can't scroll past an open loop. Line 1 raises it; the turn/payoff closes it.
- ✅ "I did not believe this cot was real." → (opens the loop: *why? is it?*) → … → "She slept outside ninety minutes." (closes it)
Don't resolve the hook in the same breath you open it; let it hang.

## 9. The authenticity test
Before any line ships, ask: **would a real person actually say this out loud, to their phone, to a friend?** If it sounds like a teleprompter or a brand, rewrite it the way a person would text it. Over-scripting kills trust faster than anything. Small imperfections (an aside, a half-thought, a "wait—") read as real.

## 10. Don't reuse signature phrases — vary every time
The persona's idiom markers and example lines show the VOICE; they are NOT phrases to paste in. The moment a construction like "I kept waiting for [the catch / the hard part / the annoying part]" appears in more than one video, it's a tell. Express the anti-climax a FRESH way each script:
- ✅ "And then… nothing." / "That was the whole step." / "I braced for a catch that never came." / "Still waiting for the hard part. There isn't one."
Pick a new phrasing each time; never lean on the same signature line twice.

## 11. Keep referents clear when you compress
A tight line with "that / this / it" must point to something the viewer JUST saw or heard. If the prior line or title doesn't set it up, name the thing instead of pointing with a pronoun.
- ❌ (alone) "I kept waiting for that to be the annoying part." ("that" = ?)
- ✅ "Snaps on one-handed. Waited for the catch — none."

## 12. Production constraint — no transformation shots (STRONG)
The image/video AI cannot convincingly show a product being folded, unfolded, assembled, collapsed, or set up — those motions render badly. So write the story around STATES and results, not the act:
- ✅ "Already set up in 40 seconds." / "Packs into one bag." / before → after (as two states)
- ❌ a beat that depends on watching it fold/unfold/assemble on screen
The VO can SAY the setup is fast; just don't make a shot depend on SHOWING the transformation. (Soft, for clips: the object is already placed in the start frame; motion applies to the placed object — gentle camera/parallax, or natural movement for continuous scenes — never a fold/assemble animation.)

## Quick self-check before shipping
- Does line 1 stop the scroll on its own AND open a loop?
- Read all lines aloud back to back — does it sound like ONE real person, escalating? (the authenticity test)
- Are there at least two short fragments?
- Is there one clear turn that closes the opening loop?
- Any word a viewer could misread in one second? (swap it)
- Did you cut the filler words?
